First and foremost, it would behoove us to consider the increasingly virtualized nature of society. While in most circumstances, my reflexive approach would be to re-materialize the pedagogical practice, the inherently intangible nature of creative computing and network culture make that a bit of a purely reactionary fancy. So then, fully embrace the dematerialization? Attempts could be made to fold this issue into the educational process, such as more explicitly using the tools deployed (Zoom, Cerberus FTP, GitHub) as examples when tackling network theory. Could also consider reaping the benefits of avatar-based virtual meeting platforms such as Gather or Wonder.

However, from the humble vantage point of a single student, it seems that one of the bigger obstacles is student engagement, or lack thereof. Closely related to the idea of students as passive objects theorized by Paolo Freire in his banking model of education, this disengagement is unfortunate for both teacher and student. While more punitive models might propose enforced engagement (e.g. calling on students by name), an alternative framework proposed by Freire considers problem-posing as a pedagogical tool. An example of this can be found in contextualized but open-ended weekly reading questions. Another example could involve similarly weekly and open-ended problem-solving exercises, centered around the elaboration of a more abstract functionality (e.g. “print visitor’s name on the page” which allows for a variety of user input as well as display approaches), solo or in groups, iteratively building on a collective project.
